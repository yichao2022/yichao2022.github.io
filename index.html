1. Introduction: The Behavioral DGP

Traditional experimental power analysis often assumes static compliance and homogeneous responses. However, in my Job Market Paper (JMP), I demonstrate that human decision-making—particularly regarding health behaviors like vaccination—is governed by dynamic intertemporal tradeoffs.

The core of our Data Generating Process (DGP) is a structural model of hyperbolic discounting. The intertemporal utility of an individual \(i\) making a choice at time \(t\) is modeled as:

\[ U_{it} = V_{i} - C_{i}(t) + \beta \delta^t B_{i} \]

Through structural estimation using our Discrete Choice Experiment (DCE) data, we find the temporal discounting parameter to be: \[\delta = 0.16\]

This reflects severe present bias. Individuals are highly sensitive to immediate waiting costs (\(C_i(t)\)), which rapidly discounts the future health benefits (\(B_i\)). Ignoring this \(\delta=0.16\) parameter leads to a fundamental misunderstanding of why participants “drop out” or delay treatment, resulting in “phantom” power estimates.

2. The MIDA Framework: Mapping Structural Logic

To bridge behavioral theory with experimental practice, we adopt the DeclareDesign framework (MIDA). We map our JMP logic onto these four pillars:

Component JMP Logic / Behavioral Digital Twin
Model (M) Populations endowed with heterogeneous trust (\(\tau_i\)) and hyperbolic discounting (\(\delta=0.16\)).
Inquiry (I) The Average Treatment Effect (ATE) on vaccination uptake, specifically focusing on “Marginal Compliers”.
Data Strategy (D) Randomized assignment to treatment arms that modify wait-time costs or information transparency.
Answer Strategy (A) Comparing Naive ITT (OLS) with Structurally-Augmented Estimators that account for the behavioral friction.

The following simulation demonstrates the “Power Gap.” When the true DGP includes present bias (\(\delta=0.16\)), a standard experiment designed with a traditional linear power assumption will be significantly underpowered.

library(dplyr)

# Simulation parameters
set.seed(20260410)

results <- data.frame(
  N = rep(n_seq, 2),

4. Conclusion: The Cost of Ignoring Present Bias

Our diagnostic pipeline reveals that ignoring present bias (\(\delta=0.16\)) results in a 30% or greater misestimation of statistical power.

This is not just a theoretical nuance; it is a pragmatic failure of experimental design. By building Behavioral Digital Twins—simulated agents that “think” and “wait” like our real-world subjects—we can design experiments that are robust to the behavioral frictions of trust and time.
